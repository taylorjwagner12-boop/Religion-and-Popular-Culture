Religion in Music & Artists

How artists use music, symbolism, and storytelling to explore morality, identity, spirituality, and belonging.

🎵 Hip Hop as Modern Spirituality

Hip hop expresses both belief and doubt simultaneously, creating a space where religious ideas feel flexible rather than fixed. Artists like Kendrick Lamar and Tupac question religious authority, call out hypocrisy, and still search for meaning and justice. This tension reflects real human experience more honestly than a perfect or rigid belief system.

Kendrick Lamar: "How Much a Dollar Cost"

Kendrick Lamar's takes a biblical lesson and puts it into a situation that feels real and relatable. Instead of hearing a sermon in church, listeners follow Kendrick as he encounters a homeless man asking for money and wrestles with whether he should help him. As the story develops, it becomes clear that the homeless man represents God, connecting directly to Matthew 25:40 and the idea that how people treat others reflects their true character. What stood out to me is that the song does not simply tell listeners what is right or wrong. Instead, it forces them to think about how they would respond in the same situation. It shows how hip hop can communicate moral and spiritual lessons in a way that feels personal and relevant to everyday life.

Travis Scott: "STOP TRYING TO BE GOD"

One of the songs that stood out to me most throughout this course was STOP TRYING TO BE GOD by Travis Scott. The title alone uses religious language to challenge ideas about ego, power, and celebrity. Throughout the song, Travis suggests that people often try to place themselves in positions of ultimate authority, whether through fame, success, or influence. Looking at it through the lens of this course, I started noticing how closely it connects to celebrity culture, where artists can be treated almost like religious figures by their fans. The song critiques both that devotion and the temptation for celebrities to believe their own image. Even though it is not tied to a traditional religion, it demonstrates how religious themes continue to appear throughout modern music in conversations about identity, humility, and purpose.

Kodak Black: "Testimony"

Kodak Black's Testimony feels almost like a modern religious confession. Throughout the song, he reflects on his past mistakes, legal troubles, and personal struggles while expressing gratitude for being given another chance. The title itself is significant because testimony is often associated with sharing a story of transformation and growth. What stood out to me is how the song combines ideas of guilt, redemption, forgiveness, and gratitude, themes that appear throughout many religious traditions. Rather than pretending he is perfect, Kodak openly acknowledges his flaws and the challenges he has faced. In that way, the song becomes a reflection on personal change and shows how hip hop can be a space where artists wrestle with many of the same questions that religion has traditionally addressed.

Kanye West: "Jesus Walks"

Kanye West's Jesus Walks is probably one of the clearest examples of religion appearing directly within hip hop. Rather than separating faith from everyday life, Kanye places religious belief in the middle of real-world struggles such as violence, temptation, poverty, and social pressure. What makes the song so important is that it challenged assumptions about what mainstream rap music could discuss by making faith central to its message rather than hiding it in the background. Looking back after taking this course, it is easy to see how Jesus Walks demonstrates that popular culture does not replace religion but often becomes another space where religious questions and beliefs continue to be explored. The song shows that faith can still play a role in modern life, even in places where many people would not expect to find it.

Why Hip Hop Can Function Like Religion

Looking across all four of these songs, one thing that stood out to me is how often hip hop engages with the same questions that religion has historically tried to answer. Kendrick Lamar explores morality and compassion through a modern parable, Travis Scott warns against pride and the dangers of elevating oneself to a godlike status, Kodak Black reflects on redemption and personal transformation, and Kanye West emphasizes the role of faith in everyday struggles. Although each artist approaches these themes differently, they are all wrestling with questions about meaning, purpose, identity, guilt, forgiveness, and how people should live. Because of this, hip hop can sometimes function in ways that resemble religion by providing stories, moral lessons, symbols, communities, and frameworks for understanding personal experiences. One of the biggest takeaways from this course for me was realizing that religion has not disappeared from popular culture. Instead, many of the same religious ideas continue to appear through music, films, celebrities, and the communities that form around them, often in ways that people may not immediately recognize as religious.

⭐ Celebrity Devotion as Spiritual Practice

One thing that stood out to me throughout this course was how many of the same functions religion has traditionally provided can also be found in celebrity culture. Fans gather in communities, participate in rituals, share symbols, and build part of their identity around public figures they admire. While celebrities are obviously not religious leaders, the devotion surrounding them often reveals how people continue searching for meaning, belonging, inspiration, and connection through modern forms of culture.

Modern Spiritual Icons

🎤 Michael Jackson

Fans often treated Michael Jackson as more than just a musician. His concerts brought together massive crowds that shared symbols, traditions, and emotional experiences in ways that resemble religious gatherings. Even years after his death, fans continue visiting memorial sites, collecting memorabilia, and preserving his legacy. Looking at him through the lens of this course, it is easy to see how cultural figures can become sources of meaning, inspiration, and identity for entire communities.

✨ Taylor Swift

What stood out to me about Taylor Swift is how organized and devoted her fan community has become. Fans analyze lyrics, search for hidden messages, create traditions around album releases, and build a strong sense of belonging through their shared connection to her music. While it is obviously different from organized religion, many of the same elements are present, including symbols, rituals, community participation, and a shared story that helps people connect with one another.

🎼 Grateful Dead

The Grateful Dead created one of the clearest examples of a fan community functioning almost like a spiritual movement. Deadheads traveled from show to show, formed lasting communities, and built traditions that continued for decades. For many followers, attending concerts was about much more than music. It became a source of identity, belonging, and shared meaning. Looking back after taking this course, it reminds me of how religious communities often bring people together through common experiences and collective rituals.

👑 Beyoncé

Beyoncé's fan community demonstrates how modern celebrity culture can create powerful feelings of devotion and collective identity. Her performances often carry themes of empowerment, purpose, and transformation, while fans organize online communities and shared traditions around her work. What I find interesting is how people often describe major Beyoncé concerts as experiences rather than simply performances, showing how entertainment can sometimes provide emotional and communal connections that resemble religious gatherings.

🏀 LeBron James

LeBron James is a good example of how modern celebrities can take on roles that look similar to religious figures. Throughout his career, fans have followed his decisions, debated his legacy, worn his jerseys and symbols, and built communities around supporting him. What stood out to me after taking this course is how much meaning people attach to athletes beyond the actual sport. LeBron represents ideas like perseverance, leadership, success, and social impact, and many fans look to him as a role model whose actions carry influence beyond basketball. Looking at it through the lens of religion and popular culture, the devotion surrounding figures like LeBron shows how people continue searching for inspiration, identity, and belonging through modern cultural icons.

🎸 Elvis Presley

Elvis Presley is one of the clearest examples of celebrity devotion in modern culture. Even decades after his death, fans continue traveling to Graceland, celebrating anniversaries, collecting memorabilia, and preserving places connected to his life. What stood out to me after taking this course is how similar some of these behaviors are to religious pilgrimage and the preservation of sacred artifacts. People are not just remembering Elvis as a musician; they are participating in traditions and communities that keep his legacy alive. Looking at Elvis through the lens of religion and popular culture helped me see how people naturally create meaning, belonging, and shared identity around influential cultural figures.

🧘 Meditation, Prayer, and Spiritual Practice

Spirituality can be understood through two fundamental journeys:

  • Journey Outward to a Higher Being = Prayer (seeking connection with something greater)
  • Journey Inward to Yourself = Meditation (seeking truth within)

Phil Jackson and the Zen Bulls

Phil Jackson's use of meditation and mindfulness with the Chicago Bulls is a good example of how spiritual practices continue to appear in modern culture outside of traditional religious settings. Often referred to as the "Zen Master," Jackson encouraged players to focus on self-awareness, discipline, and being present in the moment, values that are closely connected to many religious and spiritual traditions. What makes this example interesting in the context of this course is that the practices themselves were not being used for religious worship, yet they still served many of the same functions that religion often provides. Meditation helped create a sense of unity among teammates, encouraged personal reflection, and gave players a framework for handling pressure and adversity. This demonstrates one of the major themes of the course: religion does not simply disappear in modern society. Instead, many of its ideas, practices, and goals continue to influence people through new forms and in spaces where they may not immediately be recognized as religious.

Music as Lived Religion

Concerts bring individuals together around something that feels larger than themselves. Whether it is Bob Marley's messages of peace, Kendrick Lamar's moral storytelling, or Beyoncé's empowering performances, music creates the conditions for meaningful and sometimes even spiritual experiences. People gather in these spaces, form strong connections, and adopt values that shape how they think and act. One reason this idea stood out to me is because I have seen it in my own life through my mom's relationship with music. She is incredibly devoted to live music, concerts, and festivals, not simply because she enjoys the songs, but because she feels a deeper sense of connection when she is there. For her, music creates a feeling that is difficult to replicate anywhere else and brings together community, emotion, reflection, and shared experience in a way that feels almost spiritual. She has always wanted me to attend concerts and festivals with her because she believes those experiences are about more than entertainment. Looking at it through the lens of this course, I can understand why. Much like religion, music has the ability to bring people together, create a sense of belonging, and provide moments that feel meaningful beyond everyday life. This demonstrates that while traditional religious participation may be changing, many people continue searching for transcendence, connection, and purpose through popular culture. This reinforces one of the major themes of this project: Religion has not disappeared from modern society but continues to emerge through new forms of culture, community, and shared experience.

Key Takeaways

Music Creates Meaning

Songs often explore questions about morality, purpose, identity, and personal struggle. Many artists use music to tell stories that help listeners reflect on their own values and experiences.

Artists Become Cultural Guides

Figures like Kendrick Lamar, Kanye West, and Travis Scott influence how people think about faith, redemption, responsibility, and success. Their music can function as a source of guidance in ways that resemble religious storytelling.

Community Creates Connection

Concerts, fan communities, and shared musical experiences create belonging and connection similar to religious communities. People gather around common values, symbols, and experiences that help shape their identities.
